Project Overview

The problem: 95% of global hydrogen is "grey" produced via steam reforming, emitting 12 kg of CO₂ per kg of hydrogen. That's 830 million tonnes of CO₂ annually, more than Germany's entire output. We need green hydrogen.

Our approach: A solar-powered PEM electrolysis system that produces hydrogen with zero direct emissions. Last year's team studied SOEC technology (700°C+, impractical for our setting). We built an actual working prototype using PEM at 50–80°C.

Why it matters: The DOE targets $1/kg for green hydrogen to compete commercially. Our design cuts power consumption 96% while maintaining equivalent hydrogen output, which is a meaningful step toward making green hydrogen economically viable.

How PEM Electrolysis Works

Water enters the anode side, where current splits it into O₂, protons (H⁺), and electrons. The Nafion membrane allows only protons through while blocking gases. Electrons travel through the external circuit (where we measure power). At the cathode, protons and electrons recombine into H₂. The flow channels - redesigned to be 4.5× deeper than the benchmark - are the core innovation that drove the 96% power reduction.

Technical Design

Core Innovation: Flow Channel Optimization

The benchmark PEM cell had 0.014″ deep flow channels which is barely thicker than a few sheets of paper. Water couldn't move efficiently, creating internal resistance that demanded 13W to produce a trickle of hydrogen.

My fix: I simulated and designed custom gaskets that increase channel depth to 0.0625″, 4.5× deeper. Water flows properly across 4× more electrode surface area. Less resistance means less power. We dropped input from 13W to 0.5W while maintaining the same hydrogen output.

Why PEM Over SOEC?

Previous team's research study on solid oxide electrolysis cells was interesting on paper, but impractical for the market-viable product we wanted to build and benchmark. A solid oxide cell needs to attain an operating temperatures above 600°C, which is not commercially viable and is much more difficult to measure reliable test results.

Materials Selection

  • Nafion 117: Industry-standard PEM, 0.2 S/cm conductivity (2× benchmark), stable −20°C to 120°C
  • Titanium Grade 2: Corrosion-resistant electrodes with good electrical conductivity
  • Titanium sintered mesh: Gas diffusion layer for even water/gas distribution
  • Garolite (G-10): Electrical insulator that won't ignite under laser (learned this the hard way)
  • Aluminum 6061-T6: Lightweight compression plates, easy to machine
  • Teflon-coated bolts: Prevents shorting between plates

Operating Conditions

  • Temperature: 50–80°C
  • Voltage: 5V input (both benchmark and ours)
  • Power: Benchmark 13W, ours 0.5–1W
  • H₂ output: Both plateau at 2.5×10⁴ mg/L in test chamber
  • Note: Remaining internal resistance suggests further optimization potential via better compression

Manufacturing Process

2-Week Fabrication Timeline

Week 1: Prototyping & CAM Validation

Built prototypes from cardboard (internals) and HDPE (externals/fittings) to verify fit and assembly sequence. Used this phase to develop and validate CAM programs. Had shop managers approve all toolpaths before touching actual materials.

Material-Specific Machining

Waterjet: Garolite gaskets (laser cutting ignites the fiberglass)
Wire EDM: All metal gaskets and titanium parts (CNC bits wear too fast)
CNC mill: Aluminum compression blocks
Rigid tapping: All threaded holes for bolts and NPT fittings
Manual assembly: Stacking and compression with proper torque sequence

Wire EDM toolpath: Custom flow channel geometry for titanium electrode gasket. The U-shaped channels are the optimization (4.5× deeper than benchmark). Red line shows the cutting path. Holes are bolt clearances. Wire EDM was necessary because titanium chews through steel bits.

Week 2: Production Run

Machined all final parts across five machine types. Split the team: two machinists (including me) handled fabrication while the research subgroup tested the commercial cell for baseline data. Zero safety incidents, zero scrapped parts.

Lessons Learned

Prototyping first was critical. I caught assembly interferences and tolerance stackup issues before wasting expensive materials. Also learned the hard way that Garolite smokes under laser cutting, producing toxic fiberglass particles. Waterjet was the right call.

Performance Validation

Test Methodology

Measured hydrogen concentration with an MQ-8 sensor in a sealed 0.003 m³ acrylic chamber. Both our system and the benchmark ran at 5V until concentration plateaued - indicating steady state where production equals leakage.

Economics & Scalability

Path to $1/kg Hydrogen

The DOE targets $1/kg for green hydrogen to compete with grey hydrogen ($4–6/kg today). Our contribution: reducing the energy cost per kg by 96%.

Per-unit cost at scale: Nafion 117 membrane ($125), titanium components ($190), garolite gaskets ($15), aluminum plates ($10), hardware ($15). Total: $355 per cell.

Problems We Solved

Continuous Operation

Initial tests showed inconsistent hydrogen generation and we observed that water flow was intermittent.

Fix: Identified the need for an integrated pump to regulate flow, which became a design feature in our final iteration of our testbed.

Gas Separation

H₂ and O₂ need separate collection paths.

Fix: Titanium mesh and proper gasket sealing gave the fluids complete separation. Separate outlet fittings per stream, validated with minimal O₂ ppm at the H₂ outlet.

Brittle Materials

Garolite and membrane materials are fragile. Wrong machining method destroys the part.

Fix: Material-specific strategies: waterjet for garolite, wire EDM for metals, careful manual assembly. Prototyped with cheap materials first to validate the process and analyze tolerances.

Outcomes & Future Work

What We Accomplished

  • Designed and fabricated a working PEM cell that produces hydrogen at 96% lower power than benchmark
  • Proved that optimized flow channels can dramatically cut energy requirements without sacrificing output
  • Created a manufacturable design from readily available materials and standard machining
  • Advanced renewable energy technology toward the DOE's $1/kg target

Next Steps

  • Optimize compression to drop power below 0.5W
  • Scale to multi-cell stack for higher output
  • Complete solar PV integration with MPPT controller
  • Long-term durability testing (membrane lifespan, electrode degradation)
  • Full economic analysis: actual $/kg at scale

This project demonstrated that green hydrogen is practically achievable with the right design choices, for under $500 in materials. The key was optimizing flow channels to minimize energy waste. Further refinement could make this a meaningful contributor to renewable energy infrastructure.
